
## Page 1 — Churn Problem (The Cause)

**1. Problem & Solution**
- **The Problem:** Leadership knows churn is happening (1,796 customers, ~27% rate per Page 2), but doesn't know *why* — is it price, service quality, or competitors poaching customers?
- **The Solution:** This page breaks churn into root-cause categories (Competitor, Attitude, Dissatisfaction, Price, Other) and ranks the specific reasons within each, so retention spend can be aimed at the actual leak instead of guessed at.

**2. KPIs**
- **Competitor-driven churn:** 805 customers (44.82%) — the single largest category
- **Attitude-driven churn:** 287 customers (15.98%)
- **Dissatisfaction-driven churn:** 286 customers (15.92%)
- **Price-driven churn:** 200 customers (11.14%)
- **Other/unclassified churn:** 218 customers (12.13% combined)
- **Top single reason:** "Competitor made better offer" — the longest bar by a wide margin

**3. Insights & Impact**
- **Insight 1 (Competitive exposure):** Nearly **45% of all churn** ties back to competitors offering a better deal, plan, or data allowance. This isn't a service-quality problem — it's a pricing/offer-competitiveness problem, and it's the dominant story on this page.
- **Insight 2 (Controllable churn is being ignored):** Attitude and Dissatisfaction together account for **~32% of churn (573 customers)** — and unlike competitor actions, this is 100% inside the company's control. Right now it's getting equal or less attention than the competitor narrative.
- **Insight 3 (Blind spot):** A meaningful slice of churned customers fall into "Don't know" — meaning exit-survey data isn't capturing true reasons. That's lost intelligence on roughly 1 in 14 churned accounts.

**4. Recommendations**
- **Immediate:** Build a competitive win-back/price-match offer targeted at Month-to-Month customers (the segment most exposed to competitor switching), since this single category drives 44.82% of churn.
- **Strategic:** Launch a service-attitude retraining initiative for front-line support/service staff — this is the highest-leverage *controllable* lever, addressing 287 customers' worth of churn that has nothing to do with price.
- **Systemic:** Fix the exit-survey/data-capture process so "Don't know" shrinks — without a known reason, that segment can never be targeted for retention.

## Page 2 — Churn Problem according to Age

**1. Problem & Solution**
- **The Problem:** Churn isn't evenly distributed across the customer base — but without segmentation, retention campaigns get applied as one-size-fits-all, wasting budget on low-risk groups and under-serving high-risk ones.
- **The Solution:** This page segments churn by age cohort and age bin, showing both *where the volume is* and *where the rate is dangerously high*, so retention resources can be split between "biggest bucket" and "fastest-leaking bucket."

**2. KPIs**
- **Total customers:** 6,687 | **Churned:** 1,796 | **Overall churn rate:** 26.86%
- **30–60 age group:** 1,017 churned — **56.6% of all churn**
- **Seniors:** 483 churned, but with the **highest churn rate in the dataset (52% at age bin 85)**
- **Under-30:** 296 churned — smallest segment by volume
- **Peak customer concentration:** Age bin 45 (694 customers) — the core of the customer base
- **Top reasons in this view:** Price too high, Product dissatisfaction, Service dissatisfaction (~60–73 customers each)

**3. Insights & Impact**
- **Insight 1 (Volume risk):** The 30–60 cohort is both the largest customer base and the largest churn contributor (1,017 of 1,796). Even a moderate churn rate here produces the biggest absolute revenue hit — this is the segment retention budget should weight most heavily by sheer dollars at stake.
- **Insight 2 (Rate risk):** Churn rate climbs steadily with age, from roughly 12% in the 20s to **52% at age 85**. Seniors are a smaller group in absolute terms, but they're churning at nearly 2x the company average — a structural problem (likely pricing/plan fit) that compounds as the customer base ages.
- **Insight 3 (Reason shift):** When you slice by age, the top reasons shift away from "competitor" and toward Price and Product/Service dissatisfaction — suggesting older or long-tenure customers churn more for affordability and fit than for competitive poaching.

**4. Recommendations**
- **Immediate:** Stand up a senior-focused retention outreach (60+) — proactive plan reviews and senior/fixed-income pricing tiers — to blunt the 52% churn rate at the oldest age bins before it climbs further.
- **Strategic:** Build a 30–60 loyalty/bundle program, since this segment represents over half of all churned customers — the highest-ROI place to concentrate retention spend.
- **Systemic:** Track churn rate-by-age monthly as a leading indicator (not just churn volume), so rate inflections — like the climb starting around age 60 — get caught and acted on before they show up as volume losses.

## Page 3 — Churn Analysis according to Account Length & Service Calls

**1. Problem & Solution**
- **The Problem:** Churn looks random until you ask *when in the customer lifecycle* it happens — without that, there's no way to know whether to fix onboarding, ongoing support, or contract renewal.
- **The Solution:** This page maps churn against tenure (account length) and support friction (service calls), surfacing two distinct danger windows and a leading-indicator signal the company can act on *before* a customer cancels.

**2. KPIs**
- **Month-1 churn spike:** ~600 customers churn in their very first month — by far the single largest spike on the tenure curve
- **Secondary spike:** ~170 customers churn around month 70, then the curve drops to near-zero by month 77 (contract-end effect)
- **Service call threshold:** at 0 service calls, churned customers are a small minority of the base; by **3+ service calls, churned customers outnumber retained customers** — a complete flip in ratio
- **Geographic concentration:** churn clusters heavily in central/eastern North America states

**3. Insights & Impact**
- **Insight 1 (Onboarding cliff):** Roughly **600 customers — a third of all churn — leave in month one.** This points to an onboarding/first-impression failure, not a long-term satisfaction problem. Customers are leaving before they ever get full value from the product.
- **Insight 2 (Leading indicator):** Service calls are a near-perfect early warning system. At 0–2 calls, most customers stay; at 3+ calls, **most customers churn.** That third call is effectively a tripwire the company isn't currently acting on.
- **Insight 3 (Contract-end risk):** The bump around month 70 lines up with two-year contract expiration — customers who stayed loyal for years are reconsidering exactly when they're free to leave, and currently nothing in the data suggests a renewal play is in place.
- **Insight 4 (Geographic concentration):** Churn isn't evenly spread geographically — it's concentrated in specific states, hinting at a regional service-quality or competitive-intensity issue worth investigating separately.

**4. Recommendations**
- **Immediate:** Trigger an automatic retention outreach the moment a customer logs their **2nd service call** — before the 3rd-call threshold where churn odds flip in favor of leaving.
- **Strategic:** Redesign the first-90-day onboarding journey (welcome calls, proactive check-ins, early issue resolution) to flatten the ~600-customer month-1 cliff — this is the single biggest churn event on the entire dashboard.
- **Systemic:** Launch a renewal-incentive campaign starting at month 60–65, ahead of the month-70 contract-end spike, to lock in long-tenure customers before they have a chance to shop around.
